## Executive Summary

This report compares two contrasting approaches to implementing a **$13 million reduction in total salary costs** across the UW-Madison College of Letters & Science (L&S), alongside the baseline uniform-cut scenario from the prior report. All three scenarios protect faculty positions.

| | Scenario A: Management-First | Scenario B: Management-Protected | Baseline: Uniform Cut |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Strategy** | Eliminate highest-paid management positions first | Exempt all management; cut only non-management staff | Equal % cut across all non-faculty |
| **Positions eliminated** | **58** | **~120** | **~113** |
| **Who bears the cuts** | Directors, deans, and senior managers earning >$132K | Researchers, lecturers, TAs, lab techs, support staff | All non-faculty proportionally |
| **Non-mgmt staff affected** | 0 | ~120 | ~113 |
| **Avg salary of eliminated position** | $173,700 | $83,400 | $88,200 |

**The central trade-off:** Scenario A eliminates far fewer positions (58 vs. ~120) by targeting high-salary management roles, but removes senior leadership and administrative infrastructure across the college. Scenario B preserves institutional leadership but eliminates roughly twice as many people, hitting front-line staff who deliver teaching, research, and student services.

### Consequences of Scenario A

**Advantages:**
- Fewer total people lose their jobs (58 vs. ~120)
- Preserves front-line capacity: no loss of lecturers, researchers, lab techs, advisors, or support staff
- Classroom instruction, research labs, and student advising continue at full strength
- Politically resonant: demonstrates shared sacrifice starting at the top

**Risks and costs:**
- **L&S Administration gutted.** Losing 30 of 82 management positions (36%) in the central administration eliminates most of the college's senior leadership infrastructure -- 5 associate/assistant deans, the HR director, the IT director, both facilities directors, both finance associate directors, the advancement director, the career services director, and the external relations director. Basic college operations (budgeting, HR, IT, facilities, fundraising) would be severely compromised.
- **Department-level management gaps.** Seven Department Administrator IIs and five Academic Program Directors are eliminated. These are the primary administrative leaders of academic departments -- they manage budgets, staff, compliance, and operations. Departments losing these roles would need to shift administrative burden to faculty or remaining staff.
- **Cascading workload.** Remaining management and non-management staff would inherit the responsibilities of eliminated positions, risking burnout and attrition.
- **Loss of institutional knowledge.** Senior managers tend to hold deep knowledge of university processes, compliance requirements, and external relationships.
- **Grant administration risk.** Eliminating the Research Administration Director, Research Program Directors, and financial managers could slow grant processing and compliance, putting research funding at risk.

### Consequences of Scenario B

**Advantages:**
- Preserves all management and administrative infrastructure
- Leadership continuity: no disruption to college governance, compliance, or strategic functions
- Department administrators, deans, and program directors remain in place to manage through the transition

**Risks and costs:**
- **Twice as many people lose their jobs.** ~120 positions vs. 58 in Scenario A.
- **Researchers and scientists hit hardest.** The non-management pool includes 48 Scientist IIIs ($6.0M), 19 Scientist IIs ($1.6M), 26 Scientist Is ($2.0M), 18 Researcher IIIs ($2.2M), and 108 Research Associates ($7.0M). An 8.16% cut across this pool eliminates an estimated 20+ research positions.
- **Lecturers and teaching faculty affected.** 142 lecturers ($11.7M) and 205 teaching faculty at various ranks ($21.3M) are in the non-management pool. An estimated 25+ instructional positions would be lost, directly reducing course offerings.
- **Student-facing staff reduced.** Academic advisors (41), graduate program managers (30), academic advising managers (27 -- note: these are classified as management and protected in this scenario), and similar roles would see colleagues eliminated, increasing caseloads.
- **Lab infrastructure degraded.** Instrument makers, instrumentation engineers, and lab technicians are all in the non-management pool.
- **Morale impact.** Front-line employees absorbing cuts while all management is protected creates a perception of inequity, potentially accelerating voluntary departures.

## Conclusions

Neither pure scenario is implementable as described. **Scenario A** achieves the dollar target with fewer than half the layoffs, but gutting L&S central administration of 30 senior positions would leave the college unable to perform basic functions -- budgeting, HR, compliance, IT, facilities management, and fundraising would all face critical gaps. **Scenario B** preserves leadership but requires eliminating twice as many people, and those people are the ones who actually teach classes, run labs, and serve students.

A practical approach would likely blend elements of both:

- **Target the highest-paid management positions** (e.g., above $200K) for elimination, capturing ~$2.4M from 8 positions
- **Consolidate mid-level management** through restructuring (e.g., sharing department administrators across 2-3 small departments), capturing additional savings
- **Apply a reduced uniform cut** to remaining non-faculty to close the gap, at a rate substantially lower than the 8.16% required under pure Scenario B
- **Shield front-line instructional and research positions** where possible, prioritizing roles that directly generate revenue (grant-funded researchers) or serve students (lecturers, advisors)

## Limitations

1. **Management classification is approximate.** Titles were classified as "management" based on keywords (director, dean, manager, etc.). Some titles classified as management may perform primarily technical or programmatic work.
2. **Position estimates use departmental averages.** Actual layoffs depend on which specific positions are eliminated.
3. **No consideration of funding source.** Some positions are funded by external grants and cannot be cut without forfeiting the grant. Eliminating a grant-funded research scientist saves no state dollars.
4. **Operational feasibility not assessed.** This analysis does not evaluate whether specific positions can be eliminated without violating contractual, regulatory, or accreditation requirements.
5. **No attrition modeling.** Natural turnover (retirements, resignations) could partially achieve savings without involuntary layoffs if implemented over a longer timeline.
