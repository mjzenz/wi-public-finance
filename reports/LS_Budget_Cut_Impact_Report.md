**Date:** February 2026

## Executive Summary

This report analyzes the likely impacts of a **$13 million reduction in total salary costs** across the College of Letters & Science (L&S) at UW-Madison. Total salary costs include a **30% overhead** on top of listed salaries. The cut is applied as a **uniform percentage across all departments**, and the analysis assumes **faculty positions cannot be eliminated**, meaning the entire reduction must be absorbed by non-faculty staff.

**Key findings:**

- The uniform cut percentage is **2.91%** of total salary costs.
- Because faculty positions are protected, the **effective cut to non-faculty budgets ranges from 2.9% to 27.9%** depending on the faculty-to-staff salary ratio in each department.
- An estimated **113 non-faculty positions** (~100 FTE) would need to be eliminated across L&S.
- Faculty-heavy departments in the humanities and social sciences face the most severe non-faculty impacts, with some departments potentially losing over a quarter of their staff.
- Departments with no faculty (centers, institutes, service units) face a straightforward 2.9% reduction.

### Assumptions

1. **Equal percentage cut**: Every department's total salary cost is reduced by 2.91%.
2. **Faculty positions protected**: No faculty lines can be eliminated. The entire dollar reduction in each department must come from non-faculty salary costs.
3. **Overhead is proportional**: A 2.91% cut in total salary cost is equivalent to a 2.91% cut in listed salaries (since overhead scales linearly at 30%).
4. **Salary-equivalent reduction**: The $13M cut in total costs translates to a **$10.0M reduction in listed salaries** ($13M / 1.30).
5. **Position estimates** are calculated by dividing the required salary reduction by the department's average non-faculty salary. Actual layoff numbers would depend on which specific positions are eliminated.

## Cross-Cutting Impacts

### 1. Research Enterprise Risk

L&S departments in STEM fields (Physics, Chemistry, Computer Sciences, Integrative Biology, Astronomy, Geoscience, Botany, Atmospheric & Oceanic Sciences) collectively face an estimated **~37 non-faculty position losses**. Many of these positions are research scientists, lab managers, and technical staff who directly support externally funded grants. Eliminating these positions could:

- Reduce capacity to submit and execute federal grants (NSF, NIH, DOE)
- Trigger PI departures to institutions with better research support
- Create a negative feedback loop as lost overhead recovery further tightens budgets

### 2. Instructional Capacity

Departments with large instructional missions (English, Mathematics, Computer Sciences, Psychology, Chemistry) would lose an estimated **~28 positions** combined. These departments provide high-enrollment service courses taken by students across the university. Reductions would likely result in:

- Modestly larger class sizes
- Fewer discussion sections and lab sections
- Reduced availability of some course sections
- Potential delays in course access for students in affected majors

### 3. Humanities Vulnerability

Humanities departments (History, English, Philosophy, French & Italian, German/Nordic/Slavic, Classics, Art History) face effective non-faculty cuts of **7-19%** -- roughly 2-7x the baseline rate. These departments already operate with lean staff and would face difficult choices between maintaining:

- Administrative support (scheduling, HR, finance)
- Academic advising
- Instructional support (teaching assistants, lecturers classified as academic staff)

### 4. Student Services

Student-facing units (Student Academic Affairs, Successworks, L&S Administration) would lose an estimated **~10 positions** -- staff who handle advising, career services, and administrative support for L&S's approximately 13,000 undergraduate students.

### 5. Small Unit Viability

Eleven departments/centers have 4 or fewer employees. A 2.9% cut on a 1-3 person unit translates to $2K-$15K -- far too small to eliminate a position. These units would absorb cuts through operating budget reductions, partial FTE adjustments, or merger with larger units. Their functional viability is not threatened by a cut of this magnitude.

## Limitations and Caveats

1. **Position estimates are approximate.** They are based on average non-faculty salary per department. Actual outcomes depend on which specific positions are eliminated (senior vs. junior, full-time vs. part-time).
2. **FTE vs. headcount.** Some "positions" are part-time. The estimated ~100 FTE lost is a more accurate measure of workforce reduction than the ~113 headcount figure.
3. **No revenue offsets.** This analysis does not account for potential revenue adjustments (tuition, grants, gifts) that could partially offset cuts in specific departments.
4. **Static snapshot.** Salary data reflects September 2025. Retirements, resignations, and new hires since then are not captured.
5. **Uniform cut assumption.** In practice, the college might choose to shield certain departments or functions, resulting in deeper cuts elsewhere. A differentiated approach could reduce the most extreme impacts identified in Tier 1 departments.
6. **"Faculty" defined by HR category.** The employee category "Faculty" in the data includes tenured, tenure-track, and some clinical/teaching faculty. Some academic staff classified as "Academic Staff" (e.g., lecturers, scientists) perform similar instructional or research functions but are not protected under this analysis's assumptions.
7. **Overhead rate.** The 30% overhead rate is applied uniformly. Actual fringe benefit and overhead costs vary by employee category and funding source.
